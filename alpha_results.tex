\subsubsection*{\ce{^{28}Si}, Benck, 22807.041}

\Epar{$E_n=61.5$ MeV}

    \caption{Diagnostic panels for alpha production on \ce{^{28}Si} using the Benck EXFOR dataset 22807.041 at $E_n=61.5$ MeV. The decomposition panel corresponds to the selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode=1}.}
    \label{fig:benck-en61p5-a-diagnostics}

As shown in \cref{fig:benck-en61p5-a-diagnostics}, the four \texttt{preeqmode} options are relatively close to each other over much of the outgoing-energy range where EXFOR provides data. The largest visible differences between the modes appear mainly in the low-energy peak region and near the high-energy end of the spectrum. However, the low-$E_{\mathrm{out}}$ peak is not strongly constrained by the experimental data, since EXFOR does not provide points in the lowest outgoing-energy region. Therefore, this peak should not be used as the main criterion for selecting the best pre-equilibrium mode.

The logarithmic panel is useful for inspecting the high-energy tail, where the calculated curves separate more clearly and where the TALYS predictions eventually decrease rapidly. This region must also be interpreted carefully, because the calculated cross sections become very small near the end of the spectrum. The most relevant comparison is therefore the measured shoulder and tail region, where EXFOR provides data points and where pre-equilibrium effects are expected to be more important.

The ratio plot gives a more direct view of the agreement between TALYS and EXFOR. The selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode=1}, reproduces the correct order of magnitude over the measured range, although the agreement is not uniform. In the intermediate outgoing-energy region, the ratio rises above $R=1$, reaching values of about 2, which indicates that TALYS overpredicts the experimental data in that part of the spectrum. At higher outgoing energies, approximately beyond $E_{\mathrm{out}}\sim45$--50~MeV, the ratio decreases sharply as the TALYS total becomes very small.

The component decomposition helps clarify the physical origin of this behaviour. The low-$E_{\mathrm{out}}$ peak is dominated by the compound contribution, while the region where EXFOR provides points is more strongly shaped by pre-equilibrium and other non-compound contributions. Since the tested parameter is the TALYS pre-equilibrium mode, the model selection should therefore be based mainly on the shoulder and high-energy tail rather than on the evaporation peak. For this alpha-production case, \texttt{preeqmode=1} gives the best overall compromise among the tested options.
