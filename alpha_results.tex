Observing the totals comparison in \cref{fig:benck-en61p5-a-diagnostics}, the four \texttt{preeqmode} options are very close to each other over most of the outgoing-energy range where EXFOR provides points. The main visible differences between modes occur in the low-energy peak region, which is dominated by the evaporation component and is not well constrained here due to the lack of low-$E_{\mathrm{out}}$ EXFOR points. For this reason, the logarithmic panel is more useful for inspecting the tail behaviour: the curves separate more clearly near the end of the spectrum, where the TALYS totals drop rapidly. This makes it easier to see where the high-energy end of the TALYS prediction starts to differ from the experimental trend.

To make the choice more objective, we again use the point-by-point ratio. The selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode}=1, is of the correct order of magnitude, but not uniformly close to $R\simeq 1$. The ratio increases above 1 in the mid-range, reaching a maximum of about 2, indicating an overprediction in that region. Beyond $E_{\mathrm{out}}\sim 45$--50~MeV the ratio decreases abruptly and approaches zero, because the TALYS total becomes very small while the comparison is already in the extreme high-energy end of the spectrum. This region is therefore not a reliable basis for mode selection. The components panel is particularly informative for $\alpha$ emission: it shows that the low-$E_{\mathrm{out}}$ peak is dominated by the compound contribution, while the region where EXFOR provides points is largely shaped by non-compound mechanisms. This supports selecting the mode primarily based on the shoulder/tail behaviour rather than the evaporation peak.
