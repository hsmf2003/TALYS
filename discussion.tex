\section{Discussion}

Across the analysed datasets, the agreement between TALYS and EXFOR depends strongly on both the emitted particle and the outgoing-energy region considered. For proton production, the different pre-equilibrium modes often give similar results near the main peak, while the shoulder and high-energy tail provide the clearest basis for distinguishing between configurations. This shows that the tail region is particularly important for constraining pre-equilibrium modelling.

For deuteron production, the spectra are less smooth and the discrepancies are generally larger. The TALYS calculations show sharper structures and more abrupt high-energy cut-offs than the experimental data. This suggests that composite-particle emission is more difficult to reproduce with the tested configurations, especially at the highest outgoing energies.

For alpha production, the available comparison is limited by the absence of low-outgoing-energy EXFOR points. As a result, the evaporation-dominated peak is not strongly constrained by the experimental data, and the mode selection is mainly based on the shoulder and tail behaviour.

Overall, the diagnostic plots show that model assessment should not rely only on visual agreement near the spectral peak. The ratio plots and component decompositions are essential for identifying systematic deviations and for understanding which physical mechanisms dominate different energy regions.
