\section{Methodology}

In this work, nuclear reactions were simulated with TALYS and the calculated outgoing-particle energy spectra were compared with experimental data from the EXFOR database. The comparison focused on inclusive light-ion production in neutron-induced reactions. The reactions studied were:

\begin{itemize}
    \item proton production, $(n,px)$, for several targets: Si, Fe, Cr, V and Co;
    \item deuteron production, $(n,dx)$, for silicon;
    \item alpha production, $(n,\alpha x)$, for silicon.
\end{itemize}

The incident neutron energies were chosen according to the available EXFOR datasets. Depending on the reaction and target, the analysed energies covered approximately the range from 14 MeV to 60 MeV.

The main objective was to evaluate how sensitive the calculated spectra are to the choice of pre-equilibrium model in TALYS. For this purpose, four calculations were performed for each reaction case, changing only the TALYS keyword \texttt{preeqmode} while keeping the remaining input parameters fixed. This made it possible to attribute the observed differences between calculations mainly to the treatment of pre-equilibrium emission.

The four pre-equilibrium options tested were:

\[
\texttt{preeqmode} = 1, 2, 3, 4.
\]

According to the TALYS manual, modes 1, 2 and 3 are based on the exciton model, but differ in the way the transition rates or collision probabilities are calculated. Mode 1 uses analytical transition rates with an energy-dependent matrix element. Mode 2 uses numerical transition rates with an energy-dependent matrix element and is the default TALYS option. Mode 3 also uses numerical transition rates, but with the optical model used for the collision probability. Mode 4 is different from the previous three because it is based on a multi-step direct/compound model.

In the exciton-model description, the excited nuclear system is represented by a particle-hole configuration. The exciton number is defined as

\[
n = p + h,
\]

where $p$ is the number of excited particles above the Fermi surface and $h$ is the number of holes below it. During the reaction, the system evolves through successive two-body interactions toward more complex configurations, corresponding to larger exciton numbers. Particle emission can occur before full statistical equilibrium is reached, giving rise to pre-equilibrium emission. This contribution is especially important in the intermediate- and high-outgoing-energy regions of the spectrum, above the low-energy evaporation peak.

For each reaction, incident energy and pre-equilibrium mode, the TALYS calculated total energy-differential cross-section spectrum was compared with the corresponding EXFOR spectrum. The comparison was made by analysing the peak region, the intermediate-energy shoulder and the high-energy tail. These regions are physically relevant because the low-outgoing-energy peak is usually dominated by compound-nucleus evaporation, whereas the shoulder and high-energy tail are more sensitive to pre-equilibrium emission.

In addition to the total calculated spectra, the TALYS component decomposition was also analysed. This decomposition separates the contributions associated with different reaction mechanisms, such as compound, pre-equilibrium, direct-like and multiple pre-equilibrium emission. This helped identify which mechanism dominates each region of the spectrum.

To make the comparison more quantitative, the TALYS spectra were interpolated at the outgoing-energy values of the EXFOR data points. A point-by-point ratio was then calculated:

\[
R(E_{\mathrm{out}}) =
\frac{
\left( d\sigma/dE \right)_{\mathrm{TALYS}}
}{
\left( d\sigma/dE \right)_{\mathrm{EXFOR}}
}.
\]

A value $R(E_{\mathrm{out}})=1$ indicates agreement between TALYS and EXFOR at that outgoing energy. Values above 1 indicate that TALYS overpredicts the experimental data, while values below 1 indicate underprediction.

For each case, the figures show four diagnostic panels: the comparison between TALYS and EXFOR using a linear vertical scale, the same comparison using a logarithmic vertical scale, the ratio $R(E_{\mathrm{out}})$ for all four pre-equilibrium modes, and the reaction-mechanism decomposition for the selected mode. The logarithmic-scale plots are useful for inspecting the high-energy tail, while the ratio plots make systematic deviations between model calculations and experimental data easier to identify.

The best-performing pre-equilibrium mode was selected by considering both the visual comparison of the calculated and experimental spectra and the behaviour of the ratio plot. Particular attention was given to the shoulder and high-energy tail, since these regions are expected to be most sensitive to the choice of pre-equilibrium model.
