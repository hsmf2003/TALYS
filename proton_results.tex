\subsection{Proton Production}

\subsubsection*{\ce{^{28}Si}, Benck, 22807.012}

For this dataset, TALYS simulations were analysed in the incident-energy interval
$E_n \in [28.0,50.0]\,\mathrm{MeV}$. The same comparison methodology was applied at each incident energy and for each pre-equilibrium option, \texttt{preeqmode}=1--4.

\Epar{$E_n=28.0$ MeV}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Images/EN28.0_00_2x2_diagnostics_28Si_np_Benck_22807_012.png}
    \caption{Outgoing-proton energy spectrum for neutron-induced proton production on \ce{^{28}Si}, compared with TALYS predictions for the Benck EXFOR dataset 22807.012 at $E_n=28.0$ MeV. The top panels show the comparison between the TALYS calculated energy-differential cross sections and the EXFOR data using a linear vertical scale (left) and a logarithmic vertical scale (right). The bottom-left panel shows the ratio between each TALYS calculation and the EXFOR data. The bottom-right panel shows the reaction-mechanism decomposition and the total predicted spectrum for the selected pre-equilibrium mode, \texttt{preeqmode=1}.}
    \label{fig:benck-en28-p-diagnostics}
\end{figure}

As shown in \cref{fig:benck-en28-p-diagnostics}, the best agreement with the EXFOR data is obtained mainly with \texttt{preeqmode=1} and \texttt{preeqmode=2}. These two modes reproduce the measured part of the spectrum more closely than modes 3 and 4. However, the distinction between modes 1 and 2 is not straightforward because the EXFOR data start at an outgoing energy of about $5$ MeV. As a result, the low-$E_{\mathrm{out}}$ evaporation peak is not fully constrained by experimental points.

The logarithmic panel is useful for examining the high-energy tail, but this region must be interpreted carefully when the calculated cross sections become very small or when there are few EXFOR points. The ratio panel provides a clearer comparison between the four modes. Modes 1 and 2 remain closest to $R\simeq 1$ over the better-populated part of the experimental spectrum, whereas modes 3 and 4 show larger deviations. The component decomposition shows that the low-$E_{\mathrm{out}}$ peak is dominated by the compound contribution, while the shoulder and tail are more affected by pre-equilibrium emission. Therefore, for this incident energy, modes 1 and 2 provide the best overall compromise, with \texttt{preeqmode=1} selected for the decomposition panel.

\Epar{$E_n=40.0$ MeV}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Images/EN40.0_00_2x2_diagnostics_28Si_np_Benck_22807_012.png}
    \caption{Diagnostic panels for proton production on \ce{^{28}Si} using the Benck EXFOR dataset 22807.012 at $E_n=40.0$ MeV. The decomposition panel corresponds to the selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode=1}.}
    \label{fig:benck-en40-p-diagnostics}
\end{figure}

At $E_n=40.0$ MeV, shown in \cref{fig:benck-en40-p-diagnostics}, modes 1 and 2 again provide the closest description of the experimental spectrum. Modes 3 and 4 deviate more clearly, especially in the shoulder and high-energy tail. Mode 4 gives the largest calculated cross section in part of the peak and shoulder region, leading to a stronger overprediction.

The ratio plot indicates that \texttt{preeqmode=1} gives the most stable agreement for this dataset. Although deviations from $R=1$ are still present, mode 1 remains closer to the experimental data over much of the lower and intermediate outgoing-energy range. The deviations increase toward higher $E_{\mathrm{out}}$, showing that the tail is the most sensitive region for distinguishing between pre-equilibrium modes. This behaviour is consistent with the component panel, where the compound contribution dominates the low-energy peak, while pre-equilibrium emission becomes more important in the shoulder and tail. Thus, the mode choice is not determined mainly by the peak, but by the behaviour above the evaporation region.

\Epar{$E_n=50.0$ MeV}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Images/EN50.0_00_2x2_diagnostics_28Si_np_Benck_22807_012.png}
    \caption{Diagnostic panels for proton production on \ce{^{28}Si} using the Benck EXFOR dataset 22807.012 at $E_n=50.0$ MeV. The decomposition panel corresponds to the selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode=1}.}
    \label{fig:benck-en50-p-diagnostics}
\end{figure}

At $E_n=50.0$ MeV, shown in \cref{fig:benck-en50-p-diagnostics}, the same general trend is observed. Modes 1 and 2 remain the closest to the experimental points in the main measured region of the spectrum, while the logarithmic panel is particularly useful for inspecting the high-energy tail. Since the outgoing-energy range extends further at this incident energy, the high-$E_{\mathrm{out}}$ region becomes increasingly important for distinguishing between model options.

The ratio and component panels confirm the interpretation obtained at lower incident energies. The compound contribution dominates the low-$E_{\mathrm{out}}$ peak, while pre-equilibrium emission provides the main contribution to the shoulder and tail. The largest discrepancies appear toward the high-energy end of the spectrum, indicating that this region is the most sensitive to the pre-equilibrium model. In this case, the tail should also be read carefully because the TALYS values become very small near the end of the spectrum, while the most relevant comparison remains the region where EXFOR provides measured points.

\subsubsection*{\ce{^{28}Si}, Bateman, 13740.005}

EXFOR provides incident energies in the interval $E_n\in[4,51]\,\mathrm{MeV}$. To facilitate comparison with the Benck dataset, the TALYS calculations were restricted to
$E_n=\{28.0,\,30.0,\,35.0,\,40.0,\,45.0,\,50.0\}\,\mathrm{MeV}$.
To keep the report concise and avoid repetition, only $E_n=28.0$, 40.0, and 50.0~MeV are shown here; the remaining incident energies are provided in Appendix~A.

\Epar{$E_n=28.0$ MeV}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Images/EN28.0_00_2x2_diagnostics_28Si_np_Bateman_13740_005.png}
    \caption{Diagnostic panels for proton production on \ce{^{28}Si} using the Bateman EXFOR dataset 13740.005 at $E_n=28.0$ MeV. The decomposition panel corresponds to the selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode=2}.}
    \label{fig:bateman-en28-p-diagnostics}
\end{figure}

In \cref{fig:bateman-en28-p-diagnostics}, the four modes are close in the peak region, while their differences become clearer in the shoulder and tail. This again shows that the peak is not the most useful region for selecting between pre-equilibrium models, since it is strongly influenced by compound emission and the calculated curves overlap there.

The ratio panel shows that, after the first points with larger uncertainties, the calculated spectra remain reasonably close to $R\simeq 1$ across much of the measured outgoing-energy range. The largest deviations occur at higher $E_{\mathrm{out}}$. The component decomposition supports the same physical interpretation as in the Benck case: the lower-energy part of the spectrum is mostly associated with compound emission, while the shoulder and tail are more sensitive to pre-equilibrium contributions. For this incident energy, \texttt{preeqmode=2} gives the best overall compromise and is therefore used in the decomposition panel.

\Epar{$E_n=40.0$ MeV}

    \caption{Diagnostic panels for proton production on \ce{^{28}Si} using the Bateman EXFOR dataset 13740.005 at $E_n=40.0$ MeV. The decomposition panel corresponds to the selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode=2}.}

\end{figure}

At $E_n=40.0$ MeV, shown in \cref{fig:bateman-en40-p-diagnostics}, the calculated totals overlap strongly near the peak but separate more clearly in the shoulder and high-energy tail. Modes 3 and 4 deviate more visibly from the EXFOR data, while modes 1 and 2 provide a better description of the measured spectrum.

The selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode=2}, gives a reasonable compromise over the measured range. The ratio starts at relatively high values at low outgoing energies and then becomes closer to 1 in the intermediate region. Deviations grow again toward higher $E_{\mathrm{out}}$, confirming that the tail is the most discriminating part of the spectrum. This also shows why the ratio plot is important: in the linear-scale comparison, the modes can appear similar, but the ratio plot makes systematic overprediction or underprediction easier to identify.

\Epar{$E_n=50.0$ MeV}

\begin{figure}[H]

    \label{fig:bateman-en50-p-diagnostics}
\end{figure}

At $E_n=50.0$ MeV, shown in \cref{fig:bateman-en50-p-diagnostics}, the overall agreement improves compared with lower incident energies. In this case, the ratio remains closer to 1 up to approximately $E_{\mathrm{out}}\approx 30$ MeV, indicating a better reproduction of the main part of the measured spectrum.

For the Bateman dataset, the mode selection is therefore driven less by the peak region, where the calculated totals often overlap, and more by the behaviour in the shoulder and tail. The high-energy end still shows larger deviations, but the main measured region is reproduced more coherently than at lower incident energies. For this case, \texttt{preeqmode=1} gives the best overall behaviour and is used for the component decomposition.

\subsubsection*{\ce{^{56}Fe}, Slypen, 22884.015}

The Slypen dataset provides an additional proton-production comparison for iron. The same diagnostic representation is used here, allowing the total spectra, ratio behaviour, and reaction-mechanism decomposition to be assessed for each incident energy.

\Epar{$E_n=28.5$ MeV}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Images/EN28.5_00_2x2_diagnostics_56Fe_np_Slypen_22884_015.png}
    \caption{Diagnostic panels for proton production on \ce{^{56}Fe} using the Slypen EXFOR dataset 22884.015 at $E_n=28.5$ MeV. The decomposition panel corresponds to the selected mode, \texttt{preeqmode=4}.}
    \label{fig:slypen-en28p5-p-diagnostics}
\end{figure}

As shown in \cref{fig:slypen-en28p5-p-diagnostics}, the main peak is reproduced reasonably well in order of magnitude, although the different pre-equilibrium modes produce visible differences in both peak height and shoulder behaviour. The EXFOR points in the measured range indicate that the choice of pre-equilibrium model has a clear effect above the evaporation peak.

The ratio panel shows that the selected mode provides the best compromise over the measured range, although the high-energy tail remains difficult to reproduce exactly. The component decomposition indicates that the low-energy peak receives a strong compound contribution, while the shoulder and tail are mainly constrained by pre-equilibrium emission. This confirms again that agreement near the peak alone is not sufficient for judging the quality of the model. The tail carries important information about the pre-equilibrium contribution and is therefore essential for selecting the most appropriate mode.

At $E_n=34.5$ MeV, shown in \cref{fig:slypen-en34p5-p-diagnostics}, the same general behaviour is observed. The TALYS curves reproduce the order of magnitude of the EXFOR data, but the ratio panel shows that the agreement varies across the outgoing-energy range. The shoulder and high-energy tail remain the most informative regions for distinguishing between pre-equilibrium modes.

The component plot supports this interpretation. The compound contribution is more important at low outgoing energy, while pre-equilibrium emission becomes more relevant as $E_{\mathrm{out}}$ increases. The selected mode reproduces the bulk of the spectrum with the best overall compromise among the tested options, although deviations remain near the edges of the measured range. The remaining Slypen incident energies are shown in Appendix~A.
