\section{Conclusion}

This project used TALYS to calculate energy-differential light-ion emission spectra and compared the results with selected EXFOR datasets. The comparison focused on proton production for several targets, and on deuteron and alpha production for silicon. Four pre-equilibrium configurations, defined by the TALYS option \texttt{preeqmode}, were tested in order to assess the sensitivity of the predicted spectra to different model choices.

The results show that there is no single \texttt{preeqmode} that gives the best agreement for all reactions and emitted particles. For proton production, the best-performing options are generally \texttt{preeqmode=1} and \texttt{preeqmode=2}. The default TALYS option, \texttt{preeqmode=2}, often gives a reasonable description of the data, but it is not always clearly superior to \texttt{preeqmode=1}. This shows that the default model is a useful reference calculation, but not necessarily the best choice in every proton-production case.

For deuteron production, the agreement between TALYS and EXFOR is more case-dependent. The spectra are generally less smooth than for proton production, and the discrepancies are larger, especially in the high-energy tail. In several cases, TALYS predicts sharp structures or an abrupt decrease of the cross section at high outgoing energies, while EXFOR still reports non-zero values. This suggests that composite-particle emission is more difficult to reproduce and may be more sensitive to the details of the pre-equilibrium model. For alpha production, the available comparison is more limited because only one main case was analysed. In that case, \texttt{preeqmode=1} gives the best overall agreement, although the lack of low-energy EXFOR points limits the ability to constrain the evaporation component.

Overall, the most informative comparisons are those that combine the total spectra, logarithmic-scale plots, ratio plots, and component decompositions. The total spectra show the general level of agreement, while the logarithmic-scale plots make the low-cross-section regions more visible. The ratio plots help identify systematic overprediction and underprediction, and the component decompositions clarify which reaction mechanism dominates each part of the spectrum. This combined approach makes it possible not only to select the best-performing TALYS configuration for each case, but also to understand where and why the model deviates from the experimental data.
